# Best Power Automate Courses for Beginners 2026 — Free & Paid Options Compared

Power Automate is one of the most in-demand skills in the Microsoft ecosystem. LinkedIn data shows "Power Automate" appearing in 340% more job postings in 2025 compared to 2022. Whether you're automating your own work or building it into your career, learning Power Automate is a smart bet.

But the learning landscape is messy. Microsoft's free content is scattered across Learn modules, YouTube has 10,000+ tutorials of varying quality, and paid courses range from $12 Udemy specials to $2,000 bootcamps.

Here's what's actually worth your time in 2026.

## What a Good Power Automate Course Should Cover

Before comparing options, let's set the bar. A course worth completing should teach you:

1. **Triggers and actions** — The fundamental building blocks
2. **Connectors** — Which ones are standard vs premium, and what that means for licensing
3. **Expressions** — The formula language that makes flows actually useful (string manipulation, date functions, conditional logic)
4. **Error handling** — Scope blocks, try/catch patterns, configuring run-after settings
5. **Variables and data operations** — Arrays, compose actions, parsing JSON
6. **Approvals** — The most common business use case
7. **Solutions and ALM** — How to package and deploy flows properly (most courses skip this entirely)
8. **Real-world exercises** — Not "send yourself an email" but actual business workflows

If a course doesn't cover error handling and Solutions, it's teaching you how to build toys, not production flows.

## The Learning Path That Actually Works

Based on helping hundreds of construction professionals learn Power Automate, here's the sequence that produces results fastest:

**Week 1–2:** Work through a structured course (Flowtech Blueprint, Microsoft Learn, or a top Udemy course). Don't skip exercises.

**Week 3:** Build your first real workflow — something your team actually needs. An approval flow or automated notification is a good start.

**Week 4:** Add error handling to your flow. This is where most self-taught builders plateau. If your course didn't cover Scope blocks, find a dedicated tutorial.

**Month 2:** Learn Solutions and ALM. Package your flows properly. This is the difference between "person who makes flows" and "person who builds automation systems."

**Ongoing:** Use YouTube for specific problems. Subscribe to Reza Dorrani and Shane Young for new patterns.

## Who Should NOT Take a Course

Honestly? If you only need to build one or two simple flows (a notification here, a basic approval there), a full course is overkill. Watch two YouTube tutorials and build the thing.

Courses pay off when:
- You're building automation into your role or career
- You need to build production-quality flows that other people depend on
- You want to understand *why* things work, not just *how* to click through them
- You're responsible for teaching Power Automate to your team
